Four ways we keep the river alive

Programs that turn watershed care into real, measurable miles.

River Cleanups

Monthly community work days pulling trash, tires, and debris out of the river corridor and the access points where families put in.

Riparian Restoration

Replanting native streambank buffers and removing invasives to cool the water, stabilize the banks, and rebuild fish and bird habitat.

A trained volunteer sampling network tests dozens of sites monthly for E. coli, nutrients, turbidity, and temperature — published to an open data dashboard.

Inside the work

How each mile gets restored.

River Cleanups

Trash out of the water, neighbors into the watershed.

Once a month, crews of volunteers fan out across the river corridor and the public access points to haul out trash, tires, and debris before the next storm carries it downstream.

  • Monthly community work days along the corridor and access points
  • 412 tons of debris pulled from the river by volunteer crews
  • Next up: Spring Watershed Cleanup — Saturday, May 17, 8am, Miller's Bend access

Riparian Restoration

Replant the banks, cool the water, bring the habitat back.

We replant native streambank buffers and clear out invasives so the banks hold, the water runs cooler, and fish and birds have somewhere to come back to.

  • Native buffers replanted, invasive species removed reach by reach
  • Native trees planted along 84 miles of streambank in the last five years
  • Next up: Streambank Planting Day — Saturday, June 7, 9am, Cedar Run reach

Water-Quality Monitoring

Data anyone can check, gathered by neighbors anyone can join.

Trained citizen scientists sample sites across the watershed every month for E. coli, nutrients, turbidity, and temperature — and every reading is published to an open data dashboard.

  • Monthly sampling for E. coli, nutrients, turbidity, and temperature
  • 47 monitoring sites sampled monthly by 180 trained citizen scientists
  • Every result published to an open, public data dashboard

Youth & Community Education

The river is the classroom — and the next generation of stewards.

Streamside river classrooms, school field-study days, and adult watershed workshops turn curiosity into care, building the people who will keep this river wild long after us.

  • Streamside river classrooms and school field-study days
  • Adult watershed workshops for the wider community
  • Powered by 38,000 volunteer hours contributed every year
