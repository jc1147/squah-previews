1Diagnosis & Strategic Thesis

Magnetic 3D does not have a marketing problem. You have a shelf problem and a transmission problem — and a recent self-inflicted wound on top of both. Before you commit a single dollar of spend, here is the clear-eyed read of what is actually holding you back, why traffic fell, and the one strategy that fixes all three at once.

The diagnosis: three failures, stacked

1. The migration severed a referral-fed funnel. When the site was rebuilt on WordPress, the rebuild changed the URL taxonomy while leaving the old URLs as hard 404s with no redirects. This is the textbook signature of post-migration traffic loss. Pages still sitting in Google's index — /developers/, /developers/creator-tools/, /products/desktops/, /services/production/ — now return 404 with no 301 to their live equivalents. Every backlink and every ranked old URL dead-ends, and the link equity that fed the site evaporates. It compounds from there: the www certificate does not cover the www hostname, so any backlink using www. throws a full-page browser security warning that bounces the visitor entirely; the blog is split across two platforms (/blog/ on WordPress and blog.magnetic3d.com on HubSpot, with roughly 74 posts stranded on a subdomain Google treats as a separate site); and two aged exact-match domains (glassesfree3d.com, 3dglassesfree.com) sit dead-parked, passing zero equity. Server response is slow — TTFB around 1.6s, 77% of LCP, on HTTP/1.1 with no CDN. None of this is "bad SEO hygiene." Your on-page meta, schema, H1s, and alt text are actually clean — RankMath is doing its job. The damage is architectural and migration-related, which is good news, because it is fully recoverable.

2. The product's core friction cannot be felt through a flat screen. A glasses-free 3D display's entire value is the off-the-screen "pop" — and that is precisely the one thing a 2D web page, a 2D ad, and a 2D email cannot convey. So online, a ~$8–9k Wildfire (estimated) reads as "an expensive 2D display," and finance cuts it. This is why your real competitor is not Proto, Looking Glass, or Axiom — it is plain 2D digital signage at 2–3x lower cost (a new 85" Samsung QM-series runs roughly $3,000–$5,000; your own published range is "under $3,000 to $10,000+"). When the wow factor can't be transmitted, the buyer defaults to the spec sheet — and on a spec sheet, 3D always loses.

3. There is no shelf for buyers to self-select on. Buyers shop by what they're trying to do — a wow moment for a CES booth, a flagship store, an investor day, a dark ride. Your site is organized by what Magnetic 3D makes (products / software / services). Of the roughly 12–15 use-case and vertical pages a buyer would search for, exactly one exists (/retail/, built May 2026). There are no rentals pages, no comparison pages, no per-vertical pages — even though the case-study taxonomy to hang them on already exists. So at the exact moment a creative director is doing the dark, rep-free research that now precedes 65–80% of a B2B purchase (Gartner), Magnetic 3D is invisible — and on the high-volume "holographic display" and "glasses-free 3D signage" results, you are off page one while Samsung and the holographic pack rank.

The scoreboard, measured. magnetic3d.com pulls ~438 organic visits/month from 139 keywords, and ranks on page one for almost none of its own money terms. Looking Glass pulls ~55,900/month and ranks in the top 10 for 4 of your 6 core terms (and #1 on two of them) — roughly 128× your footprint. That gap is the whole opportunity: it isn't a product problem, it's a footprint problem, and footprint is buildable.

The strategic thesis: shelf + transmission + a funnel that raises the floor

The fix is one coherent move, not three programs:

  1. Build the digital shelf (the LensCrafters model). Turn the site into self-selecting "shelf space" — a bounded set of use-case, vertical, comparison, and rental pages, each with its own H1, schema, proof, and CTA, each consuming the taxonomy you already have. The buyer walks the aisle and picks themselves.
  2. Make the wow factor transmissible. The single highest-leverage asset you can build is a 3-minute, custom-rendered "experience" video that uses parallax and camera-move to simulate standing in front of the display, paired with a 2D-vs-3D side-by-side. Built once and embedded on every shelf page, it replaces the 30-minute demo and finally lets a flat page sell depth. Video on a landing page lifts conversion by roughly 80% or more. This is the antidote to friction #2.
  3. Run a measurable paid + organic funnel that raises the floor. Recover the lost equity first (redirect map, fix www, consolidate the blog, reclaim the dead domains), then layer organic onto the shelf and paid (search plus a video-first "wow" layer) on top — all wired to HubSpot via offline-conversion import so the system optimizes to real pipeline, not cheap form-fills.
  4. Own the end-customer relationship and route rentals cleanly. Roughly 80% of demand is expected to rent first (estimated); route that intent to Lumen & Forge on deliberately non-overlapping keywords — you own buy / product / use-case, Lumen & Forge owns rental / Las Vegas — so try-before-you-buy becomes the on-ramp, not a leak.

The prize. This is not a traffic-volume play in a thin niche — it is a floor-raising play. The shelf plus a transmissible wow self-qualifies prospects so your existing high-touch pursuit motion fires only on high-fit accounts. The target is 10–20 high-value end customers per year, $30k–$1M+ orders with near-all-margin content reorders, and Magnetic 3D owning that relationship directly instead of renting it through integrators. Let Samsung spend to grow the category's top-of-funnel; you convert that demand where you are genuinely differentiated.

The bottom line: stop treating this as "we need more marketing" and treat it as what it is — a broken shelf, an untransmittable product, and a leaking funnel. Spend the first 30 days recovering the migration equity and shipping the 3-minute wow video, then build the digital shelf around it, and only then turn on paid — measured on pipeline, not clicks. Fix the shelf and the transmission, and the funnel finally has something worth pointing traffic at.

6.2 The core mechanic: HOOK → TAIL self-selection

The single hardest problem in this category is that the 3D effect cannot be felt through a flat screen. A normal "explain the product" ad fails because the medium kills the payoff. So we don't try to sell on the scroll. We run a two-asset sequence that self-qualifies the audience and concentrates spend on real intent:

  • HOOK (3–6s, sound-off, 9:16 vertical, the scroll-stopper). The hook sells the reaction, not the explanation: a crowd of phones pointing at one screen, a line forming, content visibly popping off a wall. Depth is conveyed the only way it can be on a flat feed — with lateral camera moves / parallax so the screen reads dimensional even on a phone. No logo-heavy intro; the job is to stop the thumb and make the viewer need to know what that is.
  • TAIL (the on-site 3-minute "wow" / "how it works" film). This lives on the use-case landing page, so only people who clicked through — who self-selected as curious buyers — ever see it. It carries the pseudo-experience render and the 2D-vs-3D split-screen. This asset is designed to replace the 30-minute demo call and to survive the vendor-shortlist stage where the killer objection is "I can't tell if it's actually impressive from a flat screen."

Retargeting then escalates by depth of engagement, so budget compounds on the people who watched the tail. This directly serves the raise-the-floor goal: paid spend concentrates on demonstrated intent, not raw reach.

Hard dependency: the 3-minute wow video is a gating asset. Without it, paid traffic lands on pages that still can't convey the effect, and conversion will disappoint. Do not scale spend ahead of that asset existing.

The core friction

Every competitor has the same problem; none has solved it on-site. This is the textbook analog-impossible product problem, and the proven solution is video.

How to simulate a glasses-free effect on a flat screen — the production approach

You cannot show depth that requires the physical display, so you show the parallax. The technique:

  • Lateral / arcing camera moves are the entire trick. Depth on a 2D feed is conveyed by motion parallax — when the camera tracks sideways or arcs around the scene, foreground elements shift faster than background elements, and the brain reads that differential as real depth. A static shot of a 3D display looks flat; a moving shot of the same content reads dimensional. Every hero sequence must be a camera move, never a locked-off frame.
  • Render the content "coming off the screen." Compose so the subject visibly breaks the plane of the bezel toward the viewer — the off-screen pop is your product's signature, and the camera move sells it.
  • Combine simulated render + real reaction footage. The render conveys the effect; cuts to real crowds stopping and pulling out phones convey the outcome. Interactive, eye-catching booth demos drive roughly 50% more engagement and 3–5x longer dwell — that is the defensible, citable version of the wow.
  • Caption-burn and design for sound-off. The page version runs ~3 minutes; the social hook-cut runs 3–6 seconds (see 7.8).

7.8 The social on-ramp (how cold traffic enters the funnel)

Retargeting then escalates by depth of engagement — hook-viewers get served the tail; 50%+ tail-viewers get served the AI re-render lead magnet; re-render users get the named-logo proof + quote CTA. The webinar question — favor evergreen on-site video

There is a reasonable instinct to run live webinars as a demo surface. The recommendation is to favor the on-site evergreen video over webinars:

  • The buyer journey is 65–80% complete before rep contact and 61% of buyers prefer a rep-free experience. A webinar reintroduces a scheduled, human-gated, synchronous step into a process the buyer wants to run alone and on their own clock — friction at exactly the wrong stage.
  • The evergreen video has a near-zero marginal cost per viewer and is available 24/7 at the dark-research moment; a webinar costs production + presenter time every time and reaches only those who show up live.
  • The whole point of this funnel is to replace the synchronous demo, not relocate it to a different synchronous format. A webinar is a 30-minute demo call wearing a different hat.

If you want live touch, reserve it for the bottom of the funnel — a 1:1 conversation with an already-self-qualified, high-fit account — not as a top/mid-funnel gate. The evergreen video and AI re-render carry the top and middle; humans carry only the closing 17%.
