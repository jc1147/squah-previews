The challenge

A site they paid for, and could no longer touch.

Halloran & Vint serves manufacturers and logistics operators. The work is high-trust and high-ticket, and almost every new client arrives by referral, a speaking slot, or a long-tail search that lands on one of the firm's insight articles. The website sits at the top of a slow, relationship-led funnel.

Five years ago the firm commissioned a bespoke, hand-coded marketing site from a boutique shop. It looked sharp on launch day. Then the shop reorganized, the two engineers who knew the codebase left, and the firm was holding an asset it had paid handsomely for and could no longer edit. Publishing a single insight article meant emailing a contractor, waiting on a quote, and paying by the hour. A typo in a partner's bio sat live for nine days because nobody wanted to open a billable ticket to fix it.

The bigger cost was invisible. The firm sponsored conference panels and ran the occasional search campaign, but the site had no first-party attribution. Leads arrived through a contact form and three office phone lines with no way to say which campaign, page, or call actually produced an engagement. Budget was allocated on instinct.

To get even partial visibility, a previous marketing manager had bolted on a separate analytics tool, a standalone call-tracking subscription, and a form and notification add-on. Each was its own login, its own invoice, its own renewal, and none of them talked to the others. The brief to the new marketing lead was blunt: a site the team can run itself, that loads instantly, and that finally tells us where our clients come from.

What they built on SGEN

One stack replaced the developer, and the three tools.

The firm rebuilt on SGEN as a single all-inclusive platform, no plugin tax and no suite tax. The whole platform came with the plan, with nothing to assemble à la carte. It was a rebuild, not an import: the insight library's organic equity was preserved by 301-mapping every legacy article URL.
