Pre-Roll · the in-house flagship line

Your flagship pre-roll line, built and run for you.

BatchWorks runs pre-roll as a dedicated manufacturing line: every format, every infused SKU, turned out at a disciplined ~95% yield. That 95% yield is the flagship number the rest of the shop is measured against, and there is no roll room left for you to staff.

01The two bad options

Staff a roll room, or clear a co-packer's floor.

There are two ways to make pre-roll at volume, and both let the production model, not the brand, set the ceiling. Build it in-house and you are running a roll room: semi-manual cone-fillers and a 6-10 person crew on fixed payroll whether the line runs or not. Hand it to a generic co-packer and you trade headcount for a floor, a 1,000-unit-per-SKU minimum to clear before they will touch a run, which quietly kills short strains, limited drops, and anything you want to test small.

02The line, running

One line. Every format. Back to back.

Watch the flagship swap format families and the pitch makes itself. The number that decides the economics is yield: a manual line recovers 80-85% of the flower you feed it; BatchWorks holds a measurable yield of about ~95%. On a real run, the gap between 80-85% and a ~95% yield is inventory you already own, recovered instead of swept off the floor.

Yield you can bank, format breadth, and no floor.

Move pre-roll to a dedicated line and three things change at once: the margin on every run, the breadth you can ship, and the size of the smallest batch you are allowed to make.

Yield you can bank

A flagship line held to a high recovery is margin on every run, product you already own turned into units you can sell instead of dust and breakage.

Every format, one line

Straight, infused, large-format or glass-tip, you add a format without adding a line, the breadth a single roll room never gives you.

No floor to clear

There are no minimums and no 1,000-unit floor to clear; a 24-hour pilot run and a full launch use the same line and the same discipline.

04Core formats

Straight, cones, blunts, glass-tip, large-format.

The core menu covers the formats a brand actually moves, every one running the same end-to-end path on our floor: intake and METRC compliance, biomass prep, grind, fill, Dutch Crown finish, then dual weight checks before tubing and shipping. Because that whole path is in-house, a new format is idea-to-shelf R&D, not a capital project.

05Infused formats

Infused, applied at low temperature.

Infused pre-rolls run on the same flagship line, dosed at low temperature so the concentrate keeps its terpene profile and the joint still burns even. Infusion is a controlled, weight-checked step, so an infused SKU carries the same idea-to-shelf R&D path as a straight pre-roll, prototyped and shipped on the line you already run.

  • THCa and live rosin, dosed at low temperature for an even burn.
  • Distillate for a clean, high-potency infusion at scale.
  • Shatter and crumble worked into the format without a separate shop.

06Precision & QC

Filled to a fraction of a gram, checked twice.

1

Filled to spec

Every joint is filled to spec down to a fraction of a gram, a robotic fill matched to each SKU's grind and density rather than one machine forced to handle every strain.

2

Weighed, then weighed again

A weight check at fill and a second weight check with rejection QC before it is tubed, so the fill weight holds across the run, not just on the first pull.

3

The standard a person holds

The line is a per-strain ecosystem, the robotics holding the tolerance and a human-QC touch holding the call on what ships. That pairing is why the yield number repeats instead of spiking once.

07What this replaces

A 10-person roll room becomes a 4-person crew.

Running pre-roll in-house means owning the roll room: a 6-10 person crew, the cone-fillers, the supervision, and the fixed payroll that lands whether or not the line runs. Routing it to BatchWorks replaces that 10-person operation with a 4-person dedicated crew on our floor, running your SKUs at flagship yield. You stop carrying a production department as fixed overhead, and you spend on units only when you actually produce them.

08BatchWorks vs the co-packers

What the co-packers will not put in writing.

Put BatchWorks next to the co-packers a brand actually shops and the gaps are concrete. No researched competitor publishes a yield number; BatchWorks names one and runs to it. The minimum-order co-packer makes you clear a 1,000-unit-per-SKU floor before a run; BatchWorks has no minimums, so short strains and limited drops stay viable.

The generalist co-packer is labor you hand scraps to; BatchWorks is infrastructure not labor, a per-strain ecosystem quoted as a variable per-unit cost rather than a flat batch fee, built by operators who have carried a brand.

One rate, end to end, infused or not.

Pre-roll is priced as a single variable per-unit cost that covers the whole path, intake through labeling and shipping, whether the SKU is straight or infused. The rate quoted is the rate per finished, shelf-ready unit.

One line item

No separate charges for compliance, QC or finishing; intake, fill, dual weight checks, tubing, labeling and shipping fold into the one per-unit number.

Pilot or launch, same path
